Clustering of NBA Players Using Skills Statistics¶

I. Executive Summary¶

The goal of this study is to group NBA players according to skill statistics using unsupervised clustering method. The data used were NBA player statistics per game for 2006-2019 Regular Seasons, scraped from Basketball Reference. After reducing the features into 5 principal components using Truncated SVD, K-means clustering with k=3 clusters algorithm was used due to its scaleability which is good for large data sets, and for the appropriateness of the euclidean metric for the numerical values of the skill statistics data. Three clusters were formed, which are individually coherent in terms of the associated positions and skill set. The Big Men cluster are mostly built men who are mostly in the Center and Power Forward positions and excel in blocking and rebounding. The Scorers and Playmakers cluster are composed mostly of Point Guard, Small Forward, and Shooting Guard players who excel in raking up points, assists, and steals for the team. Lastly, the Role Player cluster are composed of all-around players that do not exceptionally excel in just a few aspects.

II. Data¶

The data collected Basketball Reference were NBA player statistics per game for 2006-2019 Regular Seasons. Regular Seasons were collected instead of Play-offs since there are more data points in the former compared to the latter - 82 games for Regular Season and 28 games only for Play-offs. Also, more players are covered by Regular Seasons compared to Play-offs. The metrics gathered where Statistics per Game since this is the most common way to quantify players production over a course of a season. More than a decade of data were used to reflect careers of players, and 2006 was chosen as the starting year in honor of the start of the era when Kobe Bryant wore the number 24.

1. Scaling of Vector Space Representation¶

Need for Scaling:

Any machine learning task involves working with several features. Most of the times these features are extremely different from each other both in magnitude and meaning. What scaling does that it reduces the effects of variation among the features so that a particular feature does not skew our model. To put in simpler terms scaling ensures that we are not adding "apples and oranges", so to speak.

The general methods of Scaling:

  • Standard Scaler:

Standardization is a form of normalization where we transform each value $x$ to their $z$-score,

$$z = \frac{x-\bar x}{\sigma},$$

where $\bar x$ is the mean of that feature and $\sigma$ is the standard deviation of that feature. If you perform this on all features then all features would have a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 1. This normalization works best if the distribution is at least symmetric.

  • Min-Max Scaler:

Min-max scaling rescales the feature values into the range [0, 1] by following the transformation,

$$x' = \frac{x - x_\text{min}}{x_\text{max}-x_\text{min}}.$$

This scaling is useful if the values are bounded and there are no outliers.

For our analysis we have chosen Min-Max scaler over standard since our data was not normally distributed and also our data did not seem to possess any significant outliers.

In [12]:
class MinMax:
    def __init__(self, df):
        """Store the minimum and maximum values of each column"""
        self.min = df.min()
        self.max = df.max()
        
    def minmax(self, values):
        """Standard values per column"""
        return (values - self.min)/(self.max - self.min)

B. Dimensionality Reduction¶

Dimensionality Reduction: A typical machine learning data set is prone to many challenges and high number of features/dimensions is one of them. The term is "Curse of Dimensionality" is notorious in all of data science. Thus its in our best interest to try and reduce the dimensions of our data set without loosing substantial information. There is always a trade off between computational complexity vs the amount of information we wish to retain.

The most popular dimensionality reduction algorithms are:

  • Principal Component Analysis (PCA)
  • Singular Vector Dimensionality Reduction (SVD or Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA))
  • Non-negative Matrix factorization (NMF)

In our analysis we have used Truncated SVD which is nothing but SVD where we are restricting the number of singular values/ right singular vectors to represent our original design matrix.

Singular Vector Decomposition

The singular value decomposition of a matrix A is the factorization of A into the product of three matrices $$A = UDV^T$$ where the columns of U and V are orthonormal and the matrix D is diagonal with positive real entries. When we select only top k singular values and their corresponding singular vectors our process becomes Truncated SVD. Please note that Truncated SVD yields exactly the same results as PCA for a standard scaler normalization.

C. Unsupervised Clustering Using K-Means¶

  • K-Means Clustering

    The simplest and most popular representative-based clustering method is $k$-means clustering. In this method, the distance function is the L2-norm or the squared sum error (SSE), which is just the square of the L2 norm. The representative is chosen to be the mean of the points in the cluster.

    Why the mean? Representative-based clustering methods, with $k$ clusters, actually try to minimize the objective function $Q = \sum_{j=1}^k Q_j$ where $Q_j = \sum_{x_i \in C_j} Dist(x_i, y_j)$. For $k$-means,

    $$ Q_j = \sum_{x_i \in C_j} (x_i - y_j)^2. $$

    What should be the value of $y_j$ to minimize $Q_j$? It turns out, it's the mean.

    How are the representatives initially selected? The two most common approaches are:

    • randomly assign each data point to a cluster then compute the cluster mean;

    • pick $k$ data points and set them as the cluster representative.

      The first approach tends to make the representatives closer to the center while the other approach tends to spread them out. There are slight differences in terms of performance but either can be chosen for most cases.

      The algorithm has converged if the cluster labels of points do not change after a step. The time complexity of K-means is $O(nkid)$ where n is the number of data points, k is the number of clusters, i is the number of iterations and d is the dimension of the design matrix.

K-means was chosen due to its scaleability which is good to use for large data sets, and for the appropriateness of the euclidean metric for the numerical values of the skill statistics data.

  • Choice of Hyper-Parameters

The choice of the hyper-parameter K remains the single most important criteria for clustering. We have used a variety of internal - criterias for this purpose, as explained in the MSDS 2020 DMW Notebooks on Representative-Based Clustering.

  1. Calinski-Harabasz Index:

    The Calinski-Harabasz index (𝐶𝐻) evaluates the cluster validity based on the average between- and withincluster sum of squares. It measures separation based on the maximum distance between cluster centers, and measures compactness based on the sum of distances between objects and their cluster center. $$s(K) = \frac{B(K)/(K-1)}{W(K)/(n-K)}$$

    where $B(K)$ is the between group dispersion matrix and $W(K)$is the within-cluster dispersion matrix defined by:

    $$B(K) = \sum_{k=1}^K n_k \lvert\lvert \bar x_k - \bar x \rvert\rvert_2^2,$$ $$W(K) = \sum_{k=1}^K \sum_{x \in C_k} \lvert\lvert x - \bar x_k \rvert\rvert_2^2,$$

    with $n$ be the number of points in our data, $C_k$ be the set of points in cluster $k$, $\bar x_k$ be the center of cluster $k$, $\bar x$ be the center of all points, $n_k$ be the number of points in cluster $k$ and $n$ be the number of points. The higher the value of this measure, the more defined the clusters are.

  1. Silhouette Coefficient:

    Let $Davg^{in}_i$ be the average distance of $x_i$ to data points within the cluster of $x_i$, and $Dmin^{out}_i$ be the smallest average distance to points other than its own cluster. The silhouette coefficient $S_i$ for the $i$th data point specific to the ith object, is as follows: $$ S_i = \frac{Dmin^{out}_i − Davg^{in}_i}{\max\{{Dmin^{out}_i}, Davg^{in}_i\}} $$ The overall silhouette coefficient is the average of $S_i$. Its value is in the the range (−1,1). Large positive values indicate highly separated clustering, and negative values are indicative of some level of “mixing” of data points from different clusters. This is because $Dmin^{out}_i$ will be less than $Davg^{in}_i$ only in cases where data point $x_i$ is closer to at least one other cluster than its own cluster. One advantage of this coefficient is that the absolute values provide a good intuitive feel of the quality of the clustering.

  1. Intra-Inter cluster Distance ratio:

    We sample $r$ pairs of data points from the underlying data. We then take the ratio of the average distance of pairs $P$ that belong to the same cluster, and the average distance of pairs $Q$ that belong to different clusters: $$ Intra/Inter = \frac{\sum_{(x_i, x_j) \in P)} Dist(x_i, x_j)/\left|P\right|}{\sum_{(x_i, x_j) \in Q)} Dist(x_i, x_j)/\left|Q\right|}. $$ Small values of this measure indicate better clustering behavior.

  1. Sum of squares distances to centroids: In the case of $k$-means and other representative-based methods, this is $Q$ and corresponds to the sum of squares distances to the representative points. This is given by the inertia_ property of KMeans. Smaller values suggest better clustering. This is optimized for distance-based algorithms.

def intra_to_inter(X, y, dist, r):
    """Compute intracluster to intercluster distance ratio
    
    Parameters
    ----------
    X : array
        Design matrix with each row corresponding to a point
    y : array
        Class label of each point
    dist : callable
        Distance between two points. It should accept two arrays, each 
        corresponding to the coordinates of each point
    r : integer
        Number of pairs to sample
        
    Returns
    -------
    ratio : float
        Intracluster to intercluster distance ratio
    """
    dist_P = []; dist_Q = []
    np.random.seed(11)
    for i, j in np.random.randint(low=0, high=len(y), size=[r,2]):
        if i == j:
            continue
        elif y[i] == y[j]:
            dist_P.append(dist(X[i], X[j]))
        else:
            dist_Q.append(dist(X[i], X[j]))
    ratio = np.mean(dist_P)/np.mean(dist_Q)
    return ratio

def cluster_range(X, clusterer, k_start, k_stop, actual=None):
    """Compute cluster labels, and internal and external validation values
    for different number of clusters using k-means clustering.
    
    Parameters
    ----------
    X : array
        Design matrix
    clusterer : 
        Clustering object
    k_start : int
        Minimum number of clusters
    k_stop : int
        Maximum number of clusters
    actual : array
        Ground-truth labels
        
    Returns
    -------
    res : dict
        Dictionary of internal validation valuesre lists of results for
        different k's.
    """
    
    ys = []
    inertias = []; chs = []; iidrs = []; scs = []
    ps = []; amis = []; ars = []

    for k in range(k_start, k_stop+1):
        clusterer.n_clusters = k
        
        # Cluster labels
        y = clusterer.fit_predict(X)

        # Internal validation values
        inertias.append(clusterer.inertia_)
        chs.append(calinski_harabaz_score(X, y))
        np.random.seed(11)
        iidrs.append(intra_to_inter(X, y, euclidean, 50))
        scs.append(silhouette_score(X, y))
        
    res =  dict(zip(['Sum of Squared Error', 'Calinski Harabasz Index',
                     'Intra/Intercluster Distance Ratio', 'Silhouette Score'],
            [inertias, chs, iidrs, scs]))

    return res

def plot_internal(validation, k_start, k_stop, k_spec):
    """Plot internal validation values
    
    Parameters
    ----------
    validation : dict
        Internal Validation Values
    k_start : int
        Minimum number of clusters
    k_stop : int
        Maximum number of clusters
    k_spec : int
        Chosen k to plot vertical line of
        
    Returns
    -------
    fig
    """
    ks = range(k_start, k_stop+1)
    fig, (ax1, ax2, ax3, ax4) = plt.subplots(1, 4, figsize=(20, 5))
    for j, ax in zip(validation, (ax1, ax2, ax3, ax4)):
        ax.plot(ks, validation[j], 'o--')
        ax.set_ylabel(j);
        ax.set_xlabel('Number of Clusters')
        ax.grid(True)
        ax.axvline(k_spec, linestyle='--', color='red')
    fig.suptitle('Internal Validation Values at Different k Clusters',
                 fontsize=20)
    fig.tight_layout(rect=[0, 0, 1, 0.9])
    plt.close()
    
    return fig

    ax.set_title("Cluster {}".format(cl), fontsize=15)

Cluster 1 was dominated by Center and Power Forward (C, PF) positions, while Cluster 2 was dominated by Point Guard, Shooting Guard, and Small Forward (PG, SG, SF) positions. No particular position dominated Cluster 0. From these observations, it can be hypothesized that the clusters could be interpreted as follows:

  • Cluster 0: Role Players
  • Cluster 1: Big Men (C, PF)
  • Cluster 2: Scorers and Playmakers (PG, SG, SF)

To check if this interpretations make sense, the stats commonly associated with the cluster labels were plotted to verify if the cluster in question indeed excels in those stats. For starters, the large stature of Big Men makes them ideal for C/PF positions since their height would be useful in blocking and rebounding.

V. Insights¶

Three clusters were formed, which are individually coherent in terms of the associated positions and skill set. The Big Men cluster are mostly built men who are mostly in the Center and Power Forward positions and excel in blocking and rebounding. The Scorers and Playmakers cluster are composed mostly of Point Guard, Small Forward, and Shooting Guard players who excel in raking up points, assists, and steals for the team. Lastly, the Role Player cluster are composed of all-around players that do not exceptionally excel in just a few aspects. This could explain why around 58% of the data points went into this cluster, as only a few could become very exceptional rebounders/blockers or scorers/playmakers.
